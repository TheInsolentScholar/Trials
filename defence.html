THE 12 TRIALS OF LUCY

The Defence

Noble Lucy, you have journeyed far and accomplished much. Like Socrates before the Athenian council, you now stand at the threshold of your final challenge: to defend your works and articulate the wisdom you have gained.

"The unexamined life is not worth living." - Socrates

In the tradition of Plato's Apology, where Socrates defended not just his actions but the principles that guided them, you must now prepare a defence of your twelve trials. This is not merely a recounting of what you have done, but a thoughtful explanation of what you have learned and how you have grown.

For each trial you have completed, you must be prepared to:

  • Explain the challenges you faced and how you overcame them
  • Share what knowledge or skills you gained from the experience
  • Reflect on how this trial has prepared you for your journey to Valencia
  • Connect your experience to the greater wisdom of life's journey

The ancient Greeks believed that true wisdom comes not just from experiencing challenges, but from reflecting upon them. In your defence, you demonstrate not just that you have completed tasks, but that you have extracted meaning from them.

Like Socrates, who claimed that the oracle called him the wisest of men because he alone knew that he did not know, acknowledge both what you have learned and what remains mysterious to you. Humility before the vastness of knowledge is itself a form of wisdom.

When you have completed all twelve trials and prepared your defence, present yourself before the Council (your parents) to make your case. Speak with the eloquence of Pericles, the wisdom of Athena, and the conviction of Demosthenes.

If your defence is deemed worthy, the gates of Valencia shall open to you, and your journey shall continue beyond the borders of home into the wider world.
